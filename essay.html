On Patience and the
Long Exposure

What photography taught me about stillness, and the discipline of waiting for the right light.

Ben Hickman · February 2025 · 8 min read

Canyon vista, southern Utah — 4-second exposure at f/11 — 2024

There is a particular kind of patience required to make a photograph in very low light. Not the passive patience of waiting — but an active, attentive stillness. You set the shutter for eight seconds, perhaps twelve, and you hold your breath not because it helps (it doesn't) but because the act of breathing becomes something you want to witness rather than perform. The world slows. The light accumulates on the sensor the way memory accumulates in a life: gradually, almost imperceptibly, until suddenly there is something to show for all the waiting.

I came to long-exposure photography late. For the first several years I shot the way most photographers do — reactively, instinctively, chasing decisive moments with an urgency that felt creative but was mostly just anxiety. I wanted to catch the world before it moved. What I learned, slowly, is that the world rarely needs catching. It is always moving, and the better question is how long you are willing to let it move before you release the shutter.

The Discipline of Waiting

A mentor of mine — a landscape photographer who had spent thirty years working in the American West — once told me that the real work of photography happens before you reach the location. You study the maps. You track the light tables. You read the reports, check the tides, estimate where the shadows will fall at a given hour in a given season. You arrive early and you sit with the place before you make a single frame.

At the time I thought this was romantic overstatement. Experienced photographers tend to mythologize their practice. But standing at the rim of a canyon on a November morning, tripod set, camera locked, watching the sky turn from iron grey to the faintest bruised amber — I understood what he meant. The photograph was already made. I was simply there to record it.

There is something humbling in this. The photographer, for all the talk of creative vision, is largely at the mercy of light. You can choose your position. You can choose your frame. But the quality of light — whether it arrives at all, whether it lingers or snaps off abruptly, whether the clouds cooperate — is entirely outside your control. You learn this eventually and it changes you. Impatience becomes a kind of noise you stop generating.

What the Sensor Collects

A long exposure does not freeze time the way a fast shutter does. It accumulates it. Water flowing over a rock face becomes silk. Stars arc in slow ellipses across the sky. A road at night becomes a river of red and white light, the individual cars dissolved into a collective motion. What the sensor collects is not a moment but a duration — a smear of time made visible.

There is an argument that this is a less honest form of photography. That the camera's truest function is to freeze the world as the eye sees it, and that anything else is manipulation. I have never found this argument convincing. The eye does not freeze the world either. It sweeps, saccades, builds a composite from fragments. Memory works the same way — accumulating, blurring, smoothing the edges until what remains is an impression rather than a record.

Long exposure photography is, in this sense, more truthful to the way we actually experience time than a frozen frame is. It admits that duration exists. That things change. That the world is not a series of discrete instants but a continuous unfolding that we can only approximate with any single image.

The Practice

In practice, long-exposure work requires you to slow your own nervous system. You cannot hurry it. There are photographers who treat their tripod as a slight inconvenience — something to be set up and broken down quickly, a necessary evil between the camera and the shot. I used to be one of them. Now I think of the tripod as the core of the practice. Setting it up properly, levelling it, locking the camera down — this is not the preparation for the photograph. It is the beginning of it.

You begin to see differently when you know you are going to hold a position for several seconds. You notice movement you would otherwise ignore. The way a single reed bends in an imperceptible breeze. The way a wave pulls back from wet sand. The way the light from a single window moves across the facade of a building. All of this will register in the final image — softened, ghosted, transformed — but you need to see it first.

This is what the long exposure has taught me more than anything else: attention is not a passive act. It requires the deliberate choice to stay with something. To not move on. To resist the reflex to check the last frame and seek the next location. To remain exactly where you are and let the light finish what it has started.
